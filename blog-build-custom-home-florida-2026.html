1. Why Build a Custom Home in Florida?

Florida's population continues to grow rapidly, and housing demand has pushed existing home prices higher than ever. Building custom gives you significant advantages:

  • You get exactly what you want — Layout, finishes, and features designed for your lifestyle, not someone else's
  • Built for Florida's climate — Hurricane-rated construction, impact windows, proper insulation for humidity and heat
  • Modern building code compliance — New homes meet the latest Florida Building Code, which means better energy efficiency and lower insurance premiums
  • Lower insurance costs — New construction with impact windows and modern roofing can save you 30-50% on homeowner's insurance compared to older homes
  • No bidding wars — You avoid competing with other buyers in a tight resale market
  • Warranty protection — New homes come with builder warranties covering structural, mechanical, and finish issues

💡 Did You Know?

In many Florida markets, building a new custom home costs the same or less per square foot than buying an existing home — and you get everything brand new, built to current code, with full warranty protection. With BuilderK, traditional homes start at just $150/sqft.

4. Financing Your Build

Financing a new construction home is different from buying an existing home. You'll need a construction loan — a short-term loan that funds the building process and converts to a traditional mortgage once the home is complete.

One-Time Close Construction Loan (Recommended)

This is the most popular option for Florida homebuyers. You close once, lock your rate before construction begins, and the loan automatically converts to a permanent mortgage when building is complete. This saves you thousands in closing costs and eliminates the risk of rate changes or re-qualification.

Requirements

  • Credit score: 680+ (some programs accept 620)
  • Down payment: 0% if you own your lot (land = equity), otherwise 10-20%
  • Licensed builder: Your GC must be licensed and lender-approved
  • Approved plans: Complete floor plans and construction budget
  • Income documentation: W2s, tax returns, bank statements

5. Permits & Florida Building Code

Every new construction project in Florida requires building permits. The permitting process varies by county and municipality, but generally includes:

  • Building permit: The main permit covering structural, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical work
  • Site plan approval: Shows the home's placement on the lot, setbacks, and drainage
  • Engineering review: Structural calculations and wind load analysis (critical in Florida)
  • Environmental review: Wetlands, flood zone, and tree removal permits if applicable
  • HOA approval: If building in an HOA community, architectural review is typically required before permitting

Florida Building Code Highlights

Florida has some of the strictest building codes in the country — and for good reason. The Florida Building Code requires:

  • Wind resistance: Homes must be engineered for the local wind speed zone (up to 185 mph in South Florida)
  • Impact protection: Impact-rated windows/doors or approved shutters required in wind-borne debris regions
  • Roof-to-wall connections: Hurricane straps and clips required to prevent roof uplift
  • Flood elevation: In flood zones, the finished floor must be elevated above the Base Flood Elevation (BFE)
  • Energy efficiency: Insulation, HVAC, and window ratings must meet energy code minimums

6. Construction Methods for Florida

Choosing the right construction method is one of the most important decisions you'll make. Here are the three main options for Florida homes:

CMU Block (Concrete Masonry Unit)

The most common construction method in Florida. Concrete block walls offer excellent hurricane resistance, fire safety, and thermal mass. They're termite-proof and stand up well to Florida's humidity. Most lenders and insurance companies prefer block construction.

Light Gauge Steel (LGS)

An emerging alternative that offers speed of construction, termite immunity, and precision engineering. LGS is lighter than block but still provides excellent structural performance. BuilderK is experienced in both CMU and LGS construction.

Wood Frame

Common in other states but less popular in Florida due to termite risk and hurricane vulnerability. Wood frame can work for interior walls and second-story construction when combined with a block first floor.

8. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not budgeting for site work: Clearing, fill dirt, utility connections, and impact fees can add $20,000–$50,000+ depending on the lot
  • Choosing a builder based on price alone: The cheapest bid often leads to change orders, delays, and quality issues. Look for experience, licensing, and transparency
  • Skipping the soil test: Florida's soil varies dramatically. A $500 soil test can prevent $50,000 in foundation problems
  • Not understanding the construction loan process: Construction financing is complex. Work with a builder who has lender relationships and can guide you
  • Ignoring Florida-specific risks: Hurricane ratings, flood zones, termite protection, and humidity management aren't optional in Florida — they're essential
  • Making too many changes during construction: Change orders after construction starts are expensive. Finalize your design before breaking ground
  • Not getting everything in writing: Specs, allowances, timelines, and payment schedules should all be documented in your builder agreement
