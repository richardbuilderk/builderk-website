The foundation

US model codes (the I-Codes)

Almost every US jurisdiction builds on the International Code Council (ICC) family of model codes, updated on a 3-year cycle. States and cities then adopt and amend them. Florida adopts them statewide as the Florida Building Code.

  • IRC — International Residential Code (1- & 2-family dwellings, townhouses)
  • IBC — International Building Code (everything else, incl. multifamily 3+ stories)
  • IECC — International Energy Conservation Code
  • IPC / IMC / IFGC — Plumbing, Mechanical, Fuel Gas
  • IEBC — Existing Building Code (remodels, additions, change of use)
  • NFPA 70 (NEC) — National Electrical Code
  • ASCE 7 — Minimum Design Loads (wind, flood, seismic) — referenced by the IBC/IRC

The current FBC is built on the 2021 I-Codes with ASCE 7-22 loads, plus Florida-specific amendments.

Statewide

Florida Building Code (FBC)

Florida has a single statewide code — the Florida Building Code, 8th Edition (2023), effective December 31, 2023 — based on the 2021 I-Codes and ASCE 7-22, with mandatory state amendments (especially for high wind and flood). Local jurisdictions may add their own amendments, so always check with the AHJ.

The volumes

  • FBC — Building · FBC — Residential · FBC — Existing Building
  • FBC — Plumbing · FBC — Mechanical · FBC — Fuel Gas
  • FBC — Energy Conservation · FBC — Accessibility
  • Florida Fire Prevention Code (NFPA-based, administered separately by the State Fire Marshal)

Products installed in Florida generally need a Florida Product Approval (FL #) or a Miami-Dade NOA — see Wind & hurricane below.

What makes Florida different — part 1

Wind & hurricane

Florida structures are designed to ASCE 7-22 wind loads. Pull the site-specific ultimate design wind speed (Vult) and exposure category for every project — don't assume.

  • Risk Category — most homes are Category II.
  • Design wind speed (Vult) — roughly 140–170 mph across the peninsula, higher toward the SE coast / Keys. Get the exact value from the ASCE Hazard Tool or the AHJ.
  • Exposure — B (suburban), C (open), D (near open water / coastal) — D drives higher pressures on waterfront lots.
  • Components & cladding — windows, doors, roofing and soffits are designed to their zone pressures.

Wind-Borne Debris Region (WBDR)

Where Vult ≥ 140 mph (and within 1 mile of the coast where ≥ 130 mph), openings must be protected against flying debris — impact-rated glazing or tested shutters — or the building must be designed for the higher internal pressures of a breached envelope. Most of coastal Florida is in the WBDR.

High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ)

HVHZ = Miami-Dade and Broward counties. The strictest regime in the country:

  • Products must carry a Miami-Dade NOA (Notice of Acceptance) and meet the TAS protocols.
  • Impact testing — large & small missile (TAS 201 / 202 / 203) for all openings.
  • Mandatory impact protection on every opening; specific roofing, fastening and inspection rules.
🌀 Openings rule of thumb
In the WBDR, every opening is impact-rated or shuttered. In the HVHZ (Miami-Dade / Broward), products must additionally carry a Miami-Dade NOA — a statewide FL # alone is not enough.

What makes Florida different — part 2

Flood & coastal construction

Buildings in a flood hazard area are designed under ASCE 24 (adopted by the FBC) and the local floodplain ordinance. Start with the FEMA flood zone and Base Flood Elevation (BFE) from the Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM).

ZoneMeaningBuild implication
XMinimal flood hazardNo NFIP elevation requirement
AE / A1%-annual-chance flood; BFE assigned (AE)Lowest floor ≥ DFE; flood openings & flood-resistant materials below
Coastal A / LiMWAModerate wave action (1.5–3 ft)Treated like V-zone per ASCE 24
VE / VCoastal high hazard; waves ≥ 3 ftOpen pile/column foundation, breakaway walls, engineer certification

A / AE zone

  • Lowest floor at or above the Design Flood Elevation (DFE = BFE + freeboard).
  • Enclosures below DFE need flood openings / vents (≈ 1 in² per ft² of enclosed area).
  • Flood-damage-resistant materials below DFE; no living space below.

V / VE zone (& Coastal A)

  • Open foundation — pilings or columns; no solid perimeter walls.
  • Bottom of the lowest horizontal structural member ≥ DFE.
  • Breakaway walls only below; no fill for structural support; V-zone certification by the engineer of record.
📐 Always on file
A FEMA Elevation Certificate documents the as-built lowest-floor elevation for the permit and for flood insurance. On remodels, the 50% / substantial-improvement rule can trigger full flood compliance — check before scoping.
Special conditions

Waterfront & coastal-specific work

Coastal Construction Control Line (CCCL)

Construction seaward of the CCCL needs a permit from the Florida DEP in addition to the local permit, and meets stricter standards — foundations (pilings) designed for storm scour and a 100-year storm event, and siting outside the 30-year erosion projection. Confirm the CCCL early; it can reshape the whole foundation design.

Permits beyond the building department

  • Seawalls, docks, piers, boat lifts — Florida DEP, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, and the regional Water Management District.
  • Wetlands / mangroves — protected; trimming and impacts are regulated.
  • Erosion & sediment control — NPDES stormwater permit + silt fence on disturbed sites.

Build details that change on the water

  • Sea-turtle lighting — coastal ordinances require shielded, long-wavelength (amber) exterior lighting in nesting season.
  • Corrosion — salt air demands stainless or hot-dip-galvanized connectors, fasteners and hardware.
  • Exposure D wind pressures and open-foundation flood design usually apply together on the coast.
Performance

Energy

Homes comply under the FBC — Energy Conservation (IECC-based). Our standard path is the Florida R-405 performance method (EnergyGauge), submitted with the permit set.

  • R-405 energy calc sealed and in the permit package.
  • Blower-door (envelope) and duct-leakage testing at completion.
  • HVAC sized to Manual J / S / D, not rules of thumb.

Per-project code check

Confirm these before design is final and before we file for permit.
  • Adopted edition + any local amendments (AHJ)
  • Design wind speed (Vult) + exposure category
  • WBDR? → opening protection plan
  • HVHZ? → Miami-Dade NOAs on every product
  • Product approvals (FL # / NOA) for windows, doors, roof, shutters
  • Flood zone + BFE from the FIRM
  • DFE (BFE + freeboard) + Elevation Certificate
  • V-zone / Coastal A? → open foundation + cert
  • Seaward of the CCCL? → DEP permit
  • Waterfront permits (DEP / USACE / WMD) if seawall/dock
  • R-405 energy calc + testing plan
  • Turtle lighting / NPDES if coastal site
