🛑 The one rule
Never cover work before it passes inspection. Concrete doesn't pour, walls don't close, and insulation doesn't get covered until the gate before it is signed off. A missed inspection means tear-out.

The tolerance standard

NAHB performance guidelines (RCPG)

Passing code is the floor, not the finish. We measure workmanship against the NAHB Residential Construction Performance Guidelines (RCPG) — the industry's most-used reference for what's acceptable and what's a defect, with 340+ guidelines across 13 construction categories (site work, foundations, floors, walls, roofs, drywall, finishes, etc.).

  • Sets expectations — we share the standard with clients up front, so "acceptable" isn't a debate.
  • Resolves concerns — a guideline (e.g. allowable concrete crack width, floor flatness, drywall finish) settles most questions objectively.
  • Drives our QC — our self-checks are built from these tolerances.
Before we call the inspector

Self-QC

We inspect our own work first — at every gate and at the key milestones. The superintendent runs the self-check before scheduling the AHJ.

The walks we never skip

  • Pre-pour walk — steel, embeds, plumbing sleeves, vapor barrier, dimensions.
  • Pre-dry-in walk — nailing pattern, flashing, window/door install & WRB.
  • Pre-drywall walk — rough-ins complete, blocking, fire/penetration sealing, photos for the record.
  • Pre-CO / quality walk — finishes against the RCPG, function test everything.
  • Client orientation walk — before handover.
To a clean handover

Punch list

The goal is a zero-defect handover — the client's walkthrough should find nothing we didn't already catch.

  • Punch items logged in BuilderK with trade, location and photo.
  • Assigned to the responsible sub with a due date; retainage holds until cleared.
  • Re-walk to verify before sign-off.
  • Defect rate is a tracked KPI — target under 2% of inspected items.
Beyond the standard set

Special inspections

  • Threshold buildings — a special inspector per the engineer of record's plan.
  • HVHZ (Miami-Dade / Broward) — added inspections plus verification that installed products match their Miami-Dade NOAs.
  • Pilings & engineered foundations — installation / load verification.
  • Trusses & engineered systems — inspected to the sealed shop drawings.
  • Private provider inspections where used — coordinate with the AHJ.
🌀 Coastal & high-wind
On HVHZ and coastal jobs, product approval verification and special inspections are not optional — see Standards & codes → Wind & hurricane.
